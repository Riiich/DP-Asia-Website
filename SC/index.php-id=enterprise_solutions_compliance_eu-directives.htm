DigitalPersona Compliance Solutions - EU Directives

EU Directives

The European Union has issued two primary directives on data protection and privacy: EU directive 95/46 on the Protection of Personal Data and EU directive 2002/58 covering Privacy and Electronic Communication (E-Privacy Directive).

Directive 95/46 requires government organizations and private businesses to prevent unauthorized disclosure of personal information, and mandates eight enforceable principles of good practice. Under the directive, personal data must be:

  • Fairly and lawfully processed.
  • Processed for limited purposes.
  • Adequate, relevant and not excessive.
  • Accurate.
  • Not kept longer than necessary.
  • Processed in accordance with the data subject's rights.
  • Secure.
  • Not transferred to countries without adequate protection.

Personal data includes both facts and opinions about the individual. It also extends to information regarding the intentions of the organizations controlling the data towards the individual.

Directive 2002/58 governs the confidentiality of communications made over public networks and the confidentiality of personal and private data in those communications. This directive complements the data protection directive 95/46 and applies to matters not covered under 95/46. For providers of electronic communications services, 2002/58 mandates two general obligations: security of services and duty to inform subscribers of electronic risks such as malware attacks, and maintenance of confidentiality of information.

All EU member states adopted legislation pursuant these directives or adapted their existing laws. Each country also has its own supervisory authority to monitor the level of protection. Failing to comply can result in civil lawsuits and fines, depending on the country.

Identity Compliance and Privacy Laws

Managing identity security and access control is fundamental to meeting both EU directives. DigitalPersona's products are critical in complying with the following guidelines in these directives:

Strong Passwords

  • Provide safeguards preventing password attacks on equipment storing or transmitting personal data.

Secure Communications

  • Protect personal data against unauthorized disclosure or access through use of encryption.

Multi-Factor Authentication

  • Ensure confidentiality of personal data stored on infrastructure equipment by requiring strong authentication for access.
  • Prevent tampering of communications infrastructure through use of multi-factor authentication.

Transaction Accountability

  • Provide fine-grained accountability and non-repudiation of change control transactions on infrastructure applications and equipment.
  • Protect personal data against accidental or unlawful destruction or accidental loss, alteration, unauthorized disclosure by tagging each data transaction with initiator's identity.
